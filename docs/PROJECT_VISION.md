# Project Vision

## Thesis

The semiconductor supply chain is complex, global, politically sensitive, and structurally uneven. A graph is useful here because the interesting questions are about relationships, concentration, dependency, and path structure rather than isolated company facts.

This project aims to build an evidence-backed graph database that helps researchers understand how semiconductor supply chains are organized, where they are fragile, and how shocks might propagate through them.

## Who This Could Be For

- independent researchers
- policy analysts
- journalists and investigators
- investors studying industrial concentration and geopolitical exposure
- strategy teams exploring supply-chain resilience

## Why This Is Worth Building

Most supply-chain views are too flat:

- company lists without relationships
- market maps without evidence trails
- trade dashboards without operational context
- filings and announcements without a unifying structure

A graph can connect those pieces:

- companies to roles
- companies to facilities
- facilities to countries
- companies to dependencies
- policies and restrictions to affected parts of the chain
- evidence to every important claim

## What A Strong Version Of This Project Could Become

In repo form, this can become a serious research asset:

- a curated, queryable map of the semiconductor ecosystem
- a place to run chokepoint and concentration analysis
- a structured archive of evidence behind dependency claims

As a product or service, it could evolve into:

- a researcher-facing exploration tool
- a supply-chain risk monitoring service
- a scenario analysis platform for disruptions, sanctions, or policy shifts
- an alerting system tied to filings, announcements, and regulatory changes

## Guiding Product Principles

- The graph should be useful even when data are incomplete.
- Important claims should always be traceable to evidence.
- The model should favor credibility over fake completeness.
- The project should support both exploratory analysis and future productization.
- v1 should be small enough to finish and strong enough to matter.

## What Success Looks Like In The Near Term

The first useful version should let someone answer questions such as:

- which firms and countries look like structural chokepoints
- which segments are the most concentrated
- where there is weak redundancy in upstream supply
- how a disruption in one geography could propagate across the graph

If the graph can do that credibly for a curated subset of the industry, the project has real value.
